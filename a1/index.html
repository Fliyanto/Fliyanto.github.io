Section 1

Question 1

What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

Response

At first glance, the eye-catching self-moving scribbles draw my attention, almost evoking a sense of curiosity about what the scribble will turn out to be or will lead towards. This element represents a good way to capture the audience’s attention, sparking a curiosity that urges further exploration. The smooth transition to a distorted title, wrapped around a minimalistic design, really creates a centre of focus that the designer wants the users to focus on. Interactive texts are also present to create immersion in interactive UI design. This immersive experience of interaction is further heightened through the help of sound. Soft, elegant, and grand sound further enhances the experience, which gives the audience an impression of a grand entrance when opening the web design.

Section 3

Question 3

What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

Response

The interactive hotspots that displayed a 3D model of Gehry’s work are where I spent the most time. These provided multi-layered interaction that was both visually captivating and offered extra background information on the creation process. These sections allow the users to interact with the model created by Gehry through a 360 view of a 3D model that you can interact with. The model isn’t just something to look at; rather, there are small, subtle buttons that you interact with, revealing the journey that was put into the design, giving the audience more context. The addition of contrasts through implementing early concepts and pictures of the completed building highlights the transition from vision to reality.

Scrolling through the story was the action I did the most. Section 5

Question 5

What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

Response

After experiencing the interactive experience first-hand, the intended primary goal of this interactive experience is to create a thorough yet approachable examination of Gehry’s design process in creating the Walt Disney Concert Hall. Not only that they intend to inform the audience regarding Gehry’s work, but they also seem to want to inspire audiences. This can be explored in how they emphasise that vision can be a reality through the addition of a contrastive section of early design and final product. The experience honours the skill and discipline of architecture by showing the Walt Disney Concert Hall as a journey rather than a plain, planned building. It aims to show the story behind the building, and the wonderful creative ideas that were poured into the planning and making.

Section 6

Question 6

How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

Response

Through the help of a well-balanced combination of interactive elements, clear narrative, structure, and visual design, the primary goal is conveyed in an approachable manner. A sophisticated tone is created by high-resolution photos and typography, and further explorations are encouraged by hotspots, videos, and sections that can be expanded. The flexible yet linear navigation strikes a balance between user autonomy and guided storytelling, much like a physical exhibition. Hover effects and visual contrasts are examples of subtle interface cues that draw attention without detracting from immersion.

Section 7

Question 7

What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time? (For how long and how many different times)

Response

The experience has the possibility of several visits; the experience appears to work best for brief to medium sessions lasting around five to fifteen minutes. By allowing for partial exploration, the segmented structure avoids fatigue and encourages revisits to uncover information and details that were overlooked. Concise text, layered multimedia, and interactive components allow for rapid digestion of information while still encouraging deeper exploration. Through repeated engagements, this experience progressively builds understanding, drawing the audience more and more into the story being told.

Section 8

Question 8

How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

Response

Instead of one long sitting, the interactive experience suggests a rhythm of slow exploration. Its layout is broken up into easily readable, stand-alone sections, each of which hints at more layers that are just waiting to be revealed while providing just enough details to make the reader feel satisfied. The idea that the engagement is not intended to be explored in a single session is shown by the use of hotspots, expandable text boxes, and subtle prompts that act as invitations to return. Rather, the website encourages repeated engagement, allowing users to return and observe new information or reconsider previous interactions from a different perspective. This method could be a homage to architectural design, where seeing an architectural design and trying to understand it on one sitting may be too much; rather, it should be taken slowly, and as you explore further, the more you can appreciate it. In addition to the segmented structure, small ‘rest areas’ can be found around the experience, where the user can dive deeper into finding information with a much different approach, such as videos, interactive models, hotspots and more.

Section 9

Question 9

What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

Response

The conventions of museum exhibitions, where carefully chosen displays are arranged to direct a visitor's attention through a planned narrative, are referenced in the experience. Similar to items preserved and exhibited in glass cases, Gehry's original architectural sketches and archival photos function as digital artefacts. At the same time, the addition of audio snippets, videos, and narration adds a documentary element, placing the pieces in a larger cultural narrative as opposed to being standalone artefacts. This reference gives the experience almost a serious tone, placing it between exhibition, a documentary and an archive. By using elements from these forms, it shows that this experience is more than your casual one, rather to show how it has been carefully designed a space meant to engage and educate its audience.

Section 10

Question 10

What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

Response

The reference to exhibitions and documentary narratives implies that my responsibility as a participant is to approach the experience with patience, curiosity, and focus, but more importantly, to enjoy what the experience provides. The website encourages visitors to take their time, just as they would in a museum, stopping to look out for hidden details or consider the bigger picture. The goal is to engage with the materials thoughtfully, not quickly or efficiently. Expandable features and interactive hotspots encourage close attention, guaranteeing that individuals who take the time to observe and engage gain a deeper understanding. The website presents the user as an active learner who is in charge of their research process in this way. The act of scrolling, clicking, and pausing becomes less about technical navigation and more about adopting a reflective mindset, mirroring the careful observation required in both research and architectural appreciation.

Section 11

Question 11

What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

Response

The most frustrating element of the interaction lies in its occasional lack of clarity and pacing. Without clear guidance, it's easy to miss some interactive elements because they're subtle or small, especially hotspots embedded in pictures. This may result in lost chances for exploration, which seems counterintuitive in a design that emphasises exploration. Furthermore, some multimedia components take a while to load, which breaks the immersion and interferes with the otherwise smooth scrolling experience. The experience itself moves a little too slowly at times, especially in parts where content revelations are heavily staged. Although the purposeful pacing is probably meant to reflect the contemplative nature of the subject, users used to quicker digital interactions may find it to be drawn out. This tension occasionally made me impatient, which prevented the experience from feeling as seamless as its design suggested.

Section 12

Question 12

What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

Response

The sense of discovery attained through interaction is the most fulfilling aspect of the experience. As if I were unearthing artefacts in a carefully curated archive, clicking on hidden hotspots to reveal Gehry's sketches, models, or behind-the-scenes materials consistently produced satisfying moments. Every new finding broadened my comprehension and changed the interaction from being a passive observation process to an active learning process. The smooth transitions between text, images, and video clips, as well as the integration of various media forms, were equally satisfying. This resulted in a coherent narrative where no element felt disconnected. There was a feeling of completion to this skillfully combined interactivity and narrative structure, as though every action—whether scrolling, clicking, or pausing—contributed significantly to the experience. The harmony between form and content ensured that my engagement was both intellectually enriching and aesthetically enjoyable, leaving me with a strong sense of fulfilment.
